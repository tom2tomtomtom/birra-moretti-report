Beer Category Trends by Market

UK deep dive + key summaries for momentum and future bet markets

UK STRATEGIC MARKET — 55% of global Moretti volumes

World lager is still winning — but the category is getting crowded

World/Mediterranean lagers now represent 27% of UK beer volume and 32.3% of value — up 65% and 78% vs pre-pandemic. The premiumisation wave that lifted Moretti is real and ongoing. But the category's success has attracted copycats, and the premium lager segment as a whole saw volume decline of 10.4% in 2024. The winners are now those who differentiate on meaning, not just Mediterranean imagery.

🧠 Key UK Consumer Behaviour Shifts

DRINKING LESS, BETTER

70% of beer consumption shifting to premium/super-premium. Over one-third of consumers report drinking less year-on-year — of those cutting back: 40% cite health, 25% saving money, 24% going out less. 19% of UK adults are now completely alcohol-free (up from 16% in 2011). "Drinking less but better" squarely benefits Moretti's premium position.

THE GUINNESS RENAISSANCE

Stout now holds 11.8% of on-trade market share (up from 8.2% two years prior). One in nine pints poured in the UK is a Guinness. 82% of 18–24-year-old stout drinkers are new to the category — driven substantially by TikTok virality and the #SplitTheG trend. A £48.4M off-trade brand driven by social culture, not just taste.

MODERATION WAVE

200 million pints of no/low beer consumed in 2025 — 2.7% of the total market. Almost one in two UK drinkers has consumed no/low in the past year (up from 22% in 2021). Guinness 0.0 leads off-trade at £48.4M; Heineken 0.0 leads on-trade. Madri 0.0% launched 2025 and is already award-winning. The table occasion naturally includes non-drinkers — a Moretti opportunity.

QUESTION 2

Perceptions of Italian Beer

Why Italian? What do consumers expect? What makes it authentic?

THE CORE INSIGHT

Italian beer is "the beer that understands food"

Unlike German beers (tradition-bound, conservative), Belgian beers (complex, occasion-specific), or American craft (extreme, experimental), Italian beer occupies a uniquely social and food-centric position. The primary driver of Italian beer choice is the conviction that these beers are made to be drunk with something — and the experience at the table is everything.

What Consumers Expect from Italian Beer

LIQUID EXPECTATIONS
  • 🍺 ABV: 4.5–5.5% sweet spot (93% preference range)
  • 👅 Taste: Fuller body and slightly sweeter than international lagers
  • 🌿 Bitterness: Moderate — never aggressive
  • 🍋 Finish: Crisp, citrus-inflected, clean
  • 🫧 Carbonation: Fine, delicate bubbles — for freshness not fizz
BRANDING EXPECTATIONS
  • 🏛️ Heritage story and family ownership narrative
  • 📍 Regional Italian identity (not generic "Italy")
  • 🌾 Local/indigenous ingredient sourcing
  • 📜 Consistent recipe — not "improved" or reinvented
  • 🎨 Distinctly Italian visual codes (human figures, regional motifs)

What Makes Italian Beer Feel Authentic?

📜 Historical Consistency

Birra Moretti literally prints "l'autentica" on its label — and the recipe has remained almost unchanged since 1859. Consumers trust brands that don't "improve" themselves for modern tastes. The label featuring the famous mustachioed man with a glass of beer is one of Italy's most recognised brand icons.

"If it's been the same since 1859, they must be doing something right."

📍 Regional Specificity

Authentic Italian beers tell stories rooted in place — not generic Italy, but specific regions: Lombardy's Alpine water, Sicily's coastal salt air, Piedmont's grain traditions. The more specific the story, the more authentic it feels. Vague "Italian sunshine" imagery now reads as generic.

"I want to know where it's from, who made it, what water they used."

🍽️ Food-First Philosophy

The most authentic marker of Italian beer identity is its inseparability from food. Italian lager's carbonation cuts through fatty foods — mozzarella, pizza, salumi. This isn't accidental: Peter Boizot specifically imported Peroni to serve at Pizza Express restaurants in 1973, establishing the pizza-and-Italian-beer pairing at the very inception of the category in Britain. Nearly half of all Birra Moretti occasions are consumed with food — a claim no competitor can match.

"Italian beer isn't for getting drunk. It's for eating better."
💬
Veronica Sica, Heineken Beer Brands Director: Mediterranean lagers are "synonymous with zest for life for many consumers because they are associated with holidays, sun, beach, and sea." Post-pandemic, when flights to Italy were restricted, consumers "closed their eyes" over a Mediterranean lager and transported themselves. The challenge now: that escapism has been commoditised — every competitor claims the same sunshine.
💡
Strategic Implication: "Mediterranean lifestyle" as a category cue has been commoditised. The next level of Italian beer authenticity lives in the specificity of the table moment — the meal, the gathering, the ritual of eating together. Brands that can own this territory will escape the generic sunshine-and-aperitif trap. Italian families spend 114 minutes per day eating and drinking (third highest in the OECD) — this is a universally understood and beloved cultural code in the UK that no Spanish competitor can replicate.

🍷 Italian Beer in the Context of Wine Culture

While 55% of Italians prefer wine, 45% lean towards beer — and beer is growing, especially among younger demographics. The relationship isn't competitive: wine owns formal occasions and celebration; beer owns casual gathering, aperitivo, and food-paired daily meals.

Italian beer has successfully positioned itself as the "democratic alternative" — equally authentic, more accessible, better suited to the increasing casualisation of the Italian table. The Slow Food movement's emphasis on gathering and food quality creates a halo that benefits premium Italian beer brands.

Battleground 1: The Right to Slow Down
Hustle Culture Backlash vs. Slow Living as Privilege · Connected to Creative Route 1
8
/10
There's a massive generational backlash against hustle culture underway — 76% of employees report burnout, Gen Z is actively rejecting "productivity anxiety," and #softlife has 10+ billion TikTok views. But a powerful counter-argument is emerging: slow living is a privilege of the economically secure. Those who can't afford to slow down — the majority globally — are increasingly calling this out.
🕊️ PRO SLOW LIFE

"We're all burned out. 59% of global workers are quiet quitting. The world is too fast, too overwhelming. Taking back your pace isn't lazy — it's survival."

49% Gen Z prioritise work-life balance. #slowliving growing 250% search interest globally. r/antiwork: 2.7M+ members.

VS
💸 PRIVILEGE COUNTER

"Slow living is a middle-class fantasy. Not everyone can choose 'quiet days' and WFH. This aesthetic is for people who don't have to worry about rent."

Working-class advocates note the irony: romanticising simplicity requires the luxury of financial stability first.

⚠️
Regional Variation: This tension lands very differently by market. In Italy and Switzerland, slow living is culturally embedded and less fraught. In Romania, Brazil, Serbia, and the UK working class, it feels like privilege messaging. A "slow life" campaign needs market-specific calibration or risks alienating exactly the audiences it hopes to reach.
✅ DO — Safe Territory
  • Position Moretti as enabling small, accessible slow moments — not an aspirational slow lifestyle
  • Focus on the specific ritual of the meal, not the broader philosophy of slowness
  • Show diverse tables — not just affluent, sun-drenched settings
  • Acknowledge that slowing down is hard — the beer is the reprieve, not the lifestyle
  • Ground in the Italian cultural truth: slow dining isn't a trend, it's a centuries-old practice
❌ DON'T — Risk Zone
  • Don't romanticise "the slow life" as a whole lifestyle choice
  • Don't show exclusively affluent, leisured settings (Tuscan villas etc.)
  • Don't lecture about slowing down or wellness — brand arrogance risk
  • Don't ignore the privilege subtext — it will be called out
  • Don't make slowness the product benefit — make the table connection the benefit

Battleground 2: Forced Connection vs. Chosen Solitude
Loneliness Epidemic Messaging vs. Introvert Rights · Connected to Creative Route 2
7
/10
The US Surgeon General declared loneliness an epidemic in 2023. 90% of Gen Z enjoy communal dining and 63% say it's great for meeting people. But there's a powerful pushback: forcing connection is its own kind of harm. Gen Z is also 3x more likely to identify as introverts, and nearly 50% choose to dine alone weekly — by choice. The risk: a brand that moralises about connection can feel preachy and exclusionary to those who prefer their own company.
🤝 PRO CONNECTION

"Loneliness is a public health crisis. Countries where people share meals report lower depression, better health outcomes. We need to gather again. The dinner table is medicine."

Harvard research confirms: shared meals = stronger social support networks. Retro dinner party culture trending among Gen Z.

VS
🧘 PRO SOLITUDE

"I eat alone by choice. It's not loneliness — it's self-care. Stop pathologising introversion. Not everyone needs to be at a communal table to feel connected."

73% of Gen Z report isolation yet 50% dine alone weekly. Solitude ≠ loneliness. Forced sociality causes its own harm.

Research Agent Signal (1,000+ TikTok items, 100 Reddit posts): The word "conviviality" appeared in our entire dataset exclusively within vocabulary lesson and language-learning content — never once in authentic social gathering, dining, or community contexts. People don't describe warmth and togetherness using that word. They show it. Any brand that tries to lecture audiences about "conviviality" is already operating in the wrong register.
💡
The Moretti Opportunity: This is actually Moretti's best creative territory — but only if it sidesteps the moral messaging. The brand shouldn't say "fight loneliness with our beer." Instead: show the warmth and generosity of the Italian table as irresistible and optional. The invitation, not the prescription. Moretti doesn't make you gather — it makes gathering better when you choose to.
✅ DO — Safe Territory
  • Show genuine, unscripted moments of warmth at the table — not performative togetherness
  • Position the brand as the facilitator of generosity, not connection itself
  • Lean into Italian table culture: the refilling of glasses, the passing of dishes, the staying late
  • Show that the table welcomes everyone — whoever is there
  • Celebrate the specific rituals of shared eating that make it special
❌ DON'T — Risk Zone
  • Don't make the brand about "solving loneliness" — it's preachy and patronising
  • Don't shame solo dining or the choice to be alone
  • Don't show forced group situations where everyone's performing connection
  • Don't use "we're all lonely" as the emotional hook — it's worn out
  • Don't show the picture-perfect Instagram dinner party — it reads as aspiration, not warmth

Battleground 3: Genuine vs. Performed Authenticity
Anti-Algorithm / De-influencing vs. Authenticity as New Aesthetic · Connected to Creative Route 3
7
/10
Gen Z is aggressively rejecting algorithm-optimised aesthetics — "beige rage," tired girl makeup, de-influencing, and anti-aesthetic movements all signal exhaustion with curated perfection. But here's the paradox: authenticity itself has become an aesthetic. De-influencing is now a monetised genre. Performing "realness" is the new status game. Any brand that tries to claim unpolished authenticity risks being called out as the most inauthentic move of all.
📵 ANTI-ALGORITHM

"I'm so tired of everything looking the same. The curated feed, the perfect table setting, the same music. Give me something real, messy, actual."

Beige rage trend: rebellion against minimalist monotony. De-influencing movement: urging people NOT to buy. Search for "authentic content" exploding.

VS
🎭 META-AUTHENTICITY CRITIQUE

"'Unfiltered authenticity' is just the new filter. Every brand now claims to be 'real.' It's all performance — even the performance of not performing."

TikTok now saturated with expertly produced "authentic" content. Audiences increasingly savvy about pseudo-authenticity. Cynicism growing.

⚠️
Critical Warning for Moretti: Moretti's strongest authentic claim is its age — the same recipe since 1859, the same label icon for decades. This is genuine heritage, not performed. But if the brand tries to claim "unpolished real life" in its advertising while being a multinational Heineken brand, it will be seen through immediately. The safer play: let the product's actual history speak — don't try to be TikTok-authentic.
✅ DO — Safe Territory
  • Lean into real, historic authenticity — the 1859 recipe, the unchanged label, the Italian roots
  • Show the everyday Italian table: messy, unfiltered, real — without announcing it's "authentic"
  • Use real people, real families, real meals — not models performing life
  • Trust the product's history to do the work — don't over-explain it
  • Be the brand that doesn't need to say it's real, because it demonstrably is
❌ DON'T — Risk Zone
  • Don't use "authentic" or "real" in your copy — the very claim undermines itself
  • Don't create deliberately rough/grainy content to look anti-aesthetic — it's transparent
  • Don't try to compete with de-influencers or anti-algorithm creators

Rustic, Unpolished Authenticity

5.8
/10 Brand Opportunity
WHAT'S REAL ABOUT THIS ROUTE

Moretti genuinely has what this route requires: a recipe unchanged since 1859, a label that's been largely the same for decades, a connection to real Italian family and village life. The cultural desire for "real" over "curated" is powerful and growing. If any beer brand can credibly claim unpolished authenticity, it's Moretti.

THE FUNDAMENTAL PARADOX

Performing authenticity is the new inauthenticity. The de-influencing movement has made audiences radically cynical about any brand claiming "realness." A Heineken-owned brand making a campaign about being "not like the others" will be called out. The more the creative tries to look unpolished, the more it will read as polished artifice.

HOW TO MAKE IT WORK
The only way this works is if Moretti doesn't try to look authentic — it simply is. Showing actual Italian nonni making dinner. Real families at real tables. Not "rustic aesthetic," but the genuine texture of Italian life. The 1859 recipe doesn't need to be performed — it just needs to be stated as fact. Let the history do the work.
UNIQUE BRAND ASSET
The mustachioed man on the label IS the authenticity marker. He's not a logo update — he's a real type. In a world of rebrand and refresh, Moretti's willingness to keep the same label for 100+ years is one of the most anti-algorithm things a brand can do. Don't modernise him. Let him be the proof.
STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION

The Creative Routes aren't mutually exclusive — they converge at the table

Route 2 (Sharing & Conviviality) is the primary territory — it's the most defensible, the most emotionally true, and the most directly aligned with the brand goal of owning "sharing a meal." But Routes 1 and 3 can be layered in: the table is where you slow down (Route 1), and everything about the table is real and unperformed (Route 3).

The unified brand idea might be: "The Italian Table" — the place where time stops, generosity flows, and nothing needs to be performed. This gives you slowness without privilege, connection without preachiness, and authenticity without artifice.
